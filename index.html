First, Understand Your Soil: The Jar Test

Before you buy any soil, let's figure out what you're working with. This simple test is the most powerful piece of information you can have. It tells you if your soil is mostly sand, silt, or heavy clay, which determines how well it drains and what you need to add to it.

How to Do the Test

  1. **Dig:** Grab a trowel and a clear mason jar. Dig down about 5-6 inches and get a sample of your soil, filling the jar about halfway.
  2. **Fill & Shake:** Fill the rest of the jar with water, leaving an inch of space at the top. Add a drop of dish soap, seal the lid tightly, and shake vigorously for a few minutes until all the clumps are gone.
  3. **Wait:** Set the jar on a level surface and leave it completely undisturbed for 24 hours. The different soil types will settle into visible layers.

Why This Matters

Understanding your soil composition helps you make the right amendments. Clay soil needs more organic matter to improve drainage, while sandy soil needs more compost to retain moisture.

Reading Your Results

You'll see distinct layers form:

  • **Bottom Layer (Sand):** The heaviest particles settle first. This layer will look gritty.
  • **Middle Layer (Silt):** Finer, smoother particles will settle on top of the sand.
  • **Top Layer (Clay):** The finest, stickiest particles will form the top layer, and the water above may stay cloudy.

If you have a very thick top layer of clay, you know you'll need to add plenty of compost to improve drainage. If it's almost all sand, you'll need more compost and topsoil to help it retain moisture.

Fill Low Spots

Add topsoil to the low-lying areas. A good mix is 60% sand, 40% topsoil. Add soil in layers, lightly tamping it down as you go to prevent major settling.

Rake & Grade

Use a large landscape rake to smooth the entire surface. Use the back of the rake to create a final, even grade. Eyeball it from different angles to check for consistency.

Tool Guide: Rent vs. Buy

Knowing which tools to buy and which to rent can save you money and storage space. Here's a simple breakdown for this project.

Tools to Buy

These are great long-term investments for any homeowner.

  • **Good Shovel & Landscape Rake:** You'll use these for countless other projects.
  • **Wheelbarrow:** Invaluable for moving soil, mulch, and more.
  • **Work Gloves & Safety Glasses:** Non-negotiable for safety.
  • **Stakes, String, Line Level:** Inexpensive and useful for many DIY projects.

Money Saver: Check Facebook Marketplace or local thrift stores for quality used tools at a fraction of the cost.

Tools to Rent

These are powerful, expensive tools you'll likely only need for a day or two.

  • **Garden Tiller:** Essential for breaking up large areas of compacted soil or old lawn. Rent this for the demolition phase.
  • **Lawn Roller:** A water-filled drum that helps compact and settle your new topsoil. Rent this for the final grading stage.

Rental Tip: Rent tools for a full day even if you think you'll only need them for a few hours. This gives you buffer time and reduces stress.

What About All These Weeds?

A yard full of weeds can feel like the biggest hurdle. Getting rid of them *before* you level is crucial, otherwise they'll just grow back through your beautiful new soil. Here are two approaches:

Option 1: The Natural Method (Sheet Mulching)

This method uses layers of organic material to smother the weeds and enrich the soil at the same time. It's eco-friendly but requires patience.

Pros:

  • Builds healthy soil biology
  • No chemicals needed
  • Adds valuable nutrients to the yard
  • Great for the environment

Cons:

  • Takes a long time (several months)
  • Requires a lot of cardboard/newspaper
  • Can be physically demanding to layer

Best for: If you have 2-3 months before you need to complete the project and want to improve soil quality naturally.

Watch Tutorial: Sheet Mulching

Option 2: The Chemical Method (Herbicide)

This involves using a non-selective herbicide to kill all existing vegetation quickly. Always read and follow the manufacturer's instructions carefully for safety.

Pros:

  • Very fast and effective (days to weeks)
  • Less physical labor upfront
  • Ensures a completely clear starting point

Cons:

  • Uses harsh chemicals
  • Can harm beneficial insects and soil life
  • Requires a waiting period before planting

Best for: If you want to complete the project quickly and are comfortable using chemical solutions.

Project Order: Level First or Fence First?

While many people build the fence first, there are excellent reasons to **level your yard *before* installing the new fence.**

Why Level the Ground First?

  • **Easier Equipment Access:** Without a fence in the way, it's much easier to get tillers, wheelbarrows, or even small bobcats into the yard to move soil efficiently.
  • **Establish Final Grade:** You can set the final, correct height and slope of your entire property. This means your fence posts will be set into ground that won't change later.
  • **Simpler Fence Installation:** Building a fence on ground that is already graded is much more straightforward than trying to build on a slope. You'll have a consistent base to work from.

Important Consideration

You must be careful not to damage your newly leveled yard during the fence installation. We'll cover tips for that in the next section!

Building Your Fence After Leveling

Now that your yard is level, you have a perfect canvas for your new fence. The key is to protect your hard work while you build.

Tips for Protecting Your New Yard

  • **Use Plywood Paths:** Lay down sheets of plywood to create temporary walkways and paths for wheelbarrows. This distributes the weight and prevents ruts and footprints in your soft, new soil.
  • **Dig Post Holes Carefully:** When you dig your post holes, place the excavated dirt onto a tarp, not directly on the ground. This makes cleanup much easier and keeps your yard tidy.
  • **Wait for Seeding:** Do not plant grass or jasmine until after the fence is completely finished. The final step of your entire project should be planting.

Pro Tip

Mark the fence line with string and stakes before you start digging. This helps ensure your fence will be perfectly straight and positioned exactly where you want it.

Fence Rules for Portland, OR

Before you build, it's crucial to know the local rules. Here is a summary of the residential fence regulations for Portland, Oregon. (Information is current as of mid-2025, but always double-check with the city for the latest updates).

Do You Need a Permit?

In most cases, **no permit is needed** if your fence meets the following height limits:

  • **Wood Fences:** 7 feet tall or less.
  • **Masonry/Concrete Fences:** 4 feet tall or less.
  • **Chain Link Fences:** 8 feet tall or less.

A permit **is required** for any fence taller than these limits, or for any barrier enclosing a swimming pool.

Height & Location Rules (Setbacks)

  • **Front Yard:** In the "front setback" (the area between your house and the street), fences are generally limited to **3.5 feet** in height.
  • **Side & Rear Yards:** In the side and rear setbacks, fences can be up to **8 feet** tall (though a permit is needed over 7 feet for wood).
  • **Property Lines:** All parts of your fence, including the concrete footings, must be entirely on your own property. It's a good idea to locate your property pins before building.

Important Note

Homeowners associations (HOAs) often have their own fence rules that may be stricter than city codes. If you're in an HOA, check their guidelines before building.

A Note on Planting Jasmine for Privacy

Planning to cover that new fence with beautiful, fragrant jasmine is a brilliant final touch! Thinking about it now will set you up for success.

When to Plant?

You should plant your jasmine **after** the fence is installed and **after** the main leveling and grading is complete. This protects the young plants from being damaged during construction and ensures the soil level around them is final.

Planting Timeline

For best results, plant jasmine in early spring or fall when temperatures are mild. This gives the plants time to establish their root systems before facing summer heat or winter cold.

Choosing the Right Jasmine:

  • **For thick privacy:** Look for a vigorous, evergreen variety like Star Jasmine (Trachelospermum jasminoides). It's not a true jasmine, but it's a fast-growing, dense vine with fragrant white flowers that's perfect for creating a living wall.
  • **Check your zone:** Make sure the variety you choose is hardy for your climate zone so it survives the winter.

Planting Tips:

  • **Enrich the soil:** When you dig the holes for your jasmine plants (along the new fence line), mix a generous amount of compost into the soil you removed. This will give the roots the nutrients they need to get established quickly.
  • **Give them space:** Plant them about 5 feet apart to give each plant room to grow and spread out. They will fill in the gaps surprisingly fast!

What Comes After Leveling?

Once the ground is level and your fence is up, you're ready for the fun part: creating your new lawn! Here's what to think about.

Seeding vs. Sod

You have two main choices for establishing your new lawn.

Grass Seed

Less expensive and offers more grass varieties, but requires more patience and consistent watering to establish.

Best for:

  • Budget-conscious projects
  • Areas with less foot traffic
  • When you have time to wait

Sod

Gives you an "instant lawn" and is great at preventing initial weed growth, but it's significantly more expensive and physically demanding to install.

Best for:

  • Immediate results needed
  • Areas with heavy foot traffic
  • Steep slopes (prevents erosion)

Hybrid Approach

Consider using sod for high-visibility or high-traffic areas (like right by the patio) and seed for the rest of the yard. This gives you the best of both worldsâ€”immediate impact where it matters most and cost savings elsewhere.

Making Daily Progress

Big projects are completed through small, consistent efforts. Here's how to make meaningful progress without burning out:

The 15-Minute Rule

On days you have no energy or motivation, commit to just 15 minutes of work. Often, once you start, you'll find the energy to continue. If not, you still made progress!

The One-Square Method

Focus on just one small square area at a time. Mark off a 5x5 foot section and complete it before moving to the next. This makes the project feel much more manageable.

The Three-Day Rule

Work for three consecutive days, then take one day off completely. This creates momentum while preventing burnout. The off day gives your body time to recover.

The Photo Journal

Take a photo at the end of each workday, even if progress seems small. Looking back at these photos will show you how much you've accomplished and keep you motivated.

Remember This

This project is a journey, not a race. Every small effort adds up. Even on days when you feel you've accomplished little, you're still moving forward. Be proud of your consistency!
